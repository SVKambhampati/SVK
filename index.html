About me

Built things. Broke things. Fixed things.
I like knowing how they actually work.

I'm a Computer Science student at Northeastern University with a concentration in AI, and I've always been more interested in how systems are built than just what they produce. I like understanding how things work under the hood, how they scale, where they break, and what it takes to make them reliable outside of a controlled environment.

That mindset has shaped the kind of work I've done. I've spent time in research labs, public-sector IT, and product teams, building across very different types of systems. At UT Dallas, I worked on distributed XR platforms, focusing on real-time performance and consistency. In a government setting, I built backend integrations and data pipelines where reliability and fault tolerance actually mattered.

More recently, I've been leading development of SentimentSignal at Oasis@Northeastern, a real-time sentiment engine that processes social media data and classifies market signals across dozens of equities using NLP. The goal is to surface shifts in sentiment early, before they show up in price action, and to do it in a way that holds up under continuous, real-time load.

Across all of this, I tend to focus on building systems that are practical, efficient, and stable in real use, not just in theory. I'm comfortable working across different parts of the stack and picking up new tools as needed to get something working end to end.

Outside of engineering, I spend a lot of time playing badminton, watching movies, and gaming, usually anything that's competitive or just a good way to reset.
